## KeyNote: The role of cybersecurity in supply chain and AI 

### Abstract:  
Supply chain security has never been more critical. Recent incidents show how a single compromised dependency can cascade across sectors in days and moved this topic from the server room to the boardroom, proving that trust is our most critical asset and our greatest vulnerability. As leaders and practitioners, we are now faced with fundamental questions: What are the essential pillars of a secure supply chain? How do we build resilience in an ecosystem we don't fully control?
This keynote will confront these challenges head-on. We will deconstruct the myth that avoiding open source is a viable path to security, arguing instead that our future depends on embracing transparency and collaborative defence. Furthermore, we will look to the horizon at the dual-edged sword of Artificial Intelligence. AI is not just another challenge; it is a transformative force that will redefine both the threats we face and the powerful opportunities we have to counter them. Join me as we explore a forward-looking strategy for securing the supply chain of tomorrow.  

*Dirk Targoni (Robert Bosch GmbH)*  

### Bio  
Dirk Targoni is an automotive cybersecurity expert focused on product security across complex vehicle ecosystems. At Robert Bosch GmbH, he serves on the Product Vulnerability and Incident Response team. 
His expertise is built upon a strong foundation in offensive security assessments through Pentesting, the rigorous standards of Common Criteria, and strategic asset management with Security Bills of Material (SBOMs). He also has a keen interest in the application of Artificial Intelligence to counter emerging threats.

## Secure AI Systems: Regulations, threats, defense mechanisms 

### Abstract:  
The EU AI Act regulates artificial intelligence (AI) within the European Union to ensure its safe and ethical use. Many safety-relevant automotive products that incorporate AI, such as road sign recognition and trajectory planning, are classified as high-risk AI systems under this legislation. As such, these products must be designed and developed “in a way that they achieve an appropriate level of cybersecurity.” A new harmonized standard that addresses the cybersecurity of AI systems has recently been drafted. It includes a comprehensive set of technical requirements, particularly focusing on the AI-specific aspects of risk assessment and security concept. In this presentation, we report on the consequences and practical questions this raises for our business, on the activities we have started to address them, and on the results we have obtained.

She is also a lecturer at Esslingen University of Applied Sciences. ## Meet ISO 18974, ISO 5230’s younger sibling! 

### Abstract:  
ISO 18974 is approaching its fourth anniversary in 2026. In that relatively short time, it has moved from a new standard on the block to a practical reference point for organizations that take product security seriously. 
This talk will walk the audience through the key elements of the ISO 18974 specification.
Using concrete examples, the session will showcase how ISO 18974 is being used today in different parts of industry – from vendors integrating it into their internal security processes and supplier requirements, to customers using it as a baseline when assessing products and services. We will discuss what has worked well, what has proved challenging, and where interpretation gaps or grey areas have emerged. 
But we will not just talk about the past, we will look toward the future as well. “What is the future role of ISO 18974 in a rapidly changing regulatory and standards landscape, and in particular in the shadow of the EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA)?” and “Is there a need to evolve ISO 18974 in light of recent advancement of generative AI?”

## The Cyber Resilience Act is coming – what developers and open source users need to do now 

### Abstract:  
The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) will become fully applicable on 11 December 2027. From that point onwards, products with digital elements placed on the EU market must comply with CRA requirements and carry the appropriate CE marking.
For many organizations, this will significantly impact how software products are developed, maintained, and documented—especially when open source components are involved.
This talk provides a practical overview of what development teams and organizations need to start doing now in order to prepare for CRA compliance and focuses particularly on the new and additional requirements introduced by the CRA and discusses how they can be integrated into existing development and open source governance processes.
Topics include:
•        Establishing a cybersecurity concept for products with digital elements
•        Applying security-by-design and security-by-default principles in architecture and development processes
•        Performing risk analysis and addressing identified cybersecurity risks
•        Managing open source dependencies and software supply chain risks
•        Creating and maintaining Software Bills of Materials (SBOMs)
•        Establishing vulnerability management and coordinated vulnerability disclosure processes
•        Providing security updates throughout the expected product lifetime
•        Preparing the necessary technical documentation for conformity assessment

## Supply Chain Security for the 99%: FOSS for trust and verifiability 

### Abstract:  
Supply chain security shouldn't require enterprise budgets or proprietary platforms. As regulations like the CRA make transparency and verifiability mandatory, the FOSS community needs accessible solutions that work for open source projects, SMEs, and large organizations alike.
This talk outlines how to build a complete end-to-end supply chain security workflow using only FOSS to provide trust and verifiability without vendor lock-in. You'll learn practical implementation strategies: which FOSS tools to deploy for scanning and analysis, how to leverage open vulnerability databases effectively, where community-curated open data fills gaps in package registries, and how open standards like PURL enable tool interoperability. We'll explain how these components integrate into actual development workflows with CI/CD pipelines, dependency review processes, and compliance reporting. And we'll cover the gaps where open solutions still need work and how the community can collaborate to fill them.
This is supply chain security built on the open source principles of transparency and verifiability, and already used in production to meet compliance requirements, manage vulnerabilities, and build trustworthy supply chains. Everything shown is deployable today, freely accessible, and built to work together rather than lock you into vendor ecosystems.

## Towards Effective LLM-based Vulnerability Detection Systems - State of Research 
  
### Abstract:  
Using Large Language Models (LLMs) for software vulnerability detection requires more than just clever prompting. In this technical deep-dive, we move beyond the basics to explore the challenges of building effective LLM-based vulnerability detection systems.
We examine suitable code representations, demonstrating why code context is essential for detecting complex security flaws. A central focus of this talk is the role of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). We present a comparative analysis of different RAG approaches, evaluating how various retrieval mechanisms and knowledge bases impact detection precision. Through technical examples, participants will learn critical architectural components and open research frontiers necessary to advance LLM-based detection systems.

## Understanding CVSS4.0 
  
### Abstract:  
In October 2023, the vulnerability scoring landscape shifted with the introduction of CVSS 4.0. While many practitioners and tools remain on version 3.1, key vulnerability sources like the National Vulnerability Database (NVD) are already migrating to the new standard. This creates a critical need for understanding and adaptation, especially within specialized fields like embedded systems and automotive.
This presentation will bridge that gap. We will demystify the CVSS 4.0 framework using practical, daily examples tailored to the unique challenges of product security. You will learn not only how to interpret the new scoring metrics but also how to effectively apply them to your own projects. Furthermore, this session will share field-tested insights from years of vulnerability rating practice, providing a solid foundation for more advanced technical deep dives and helping your organization navigate this essential transition.

## DEMO: Operational Cryptography Detection in Open Source 
  
### Abstract:  
Cryptographic risk in software is often discussed in abstract terms, yet many organisations struggle to understand where cryptography is actually implemented
within their codebases and open source dependencies. This session proposes a hands-on session focused on cryptographic detection, showing how cryptographic elements can be identified and tracked as an interactive, ongoing process rather than a one-time scan. Through a live demo, the session walks through a typical cryptographic detection workflow: analysing a source code repository and running scans using an open cryptographic detection tool. The demo highlights how detected cryptographic findings can be reviewed, confirmed, or marked as not relevant, supporting transparent decision-making while reducing noise. These classifications are stored in version-controlled files within the repository, enabling teams to re-run detections as code changes and to observe progressively clearer results over time. This approach supports a repeatable and evidence-based method for managing cryptographic awareness in open source projects without reliance on manual inventory or metadata alone.
